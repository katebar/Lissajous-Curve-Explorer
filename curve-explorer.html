Lissajous Curves

Understanding Sine Curves

In order to understand Lissajous curves, it's important to solidify our understanding of sine curves and their corresponding equations.

We can draw a sine curve with the following equation:
y(t) = Asin(2πft + δ) = Asin(ωt + δ)

Wherein:

  • A is amplitude (the peak distance of the wave function from zero)
  • 2πf, or ω, is angular frequency (number_of_circular_rotations * 2π / sec, or the angle covered per second)
  • t is time
  • δ is phase shift (or where an oscillation is at t=0)

Applying this Knowledge to Lissajous Curves

Lissajous curves are formed via a system of parametric sine equations (in other words, sine equations for which we can change certain parameters, or inputs):

x(t) = Asin(at+δ),
y(t) = Bsin(bt)

Unlike our original sine equation, the x and y coordinates of a Lissajous curve are generated by two different functions of time, wherein:

  • A and B are amplitude
  • a and b are angular frequency
  • t is time
  • δ is phase shift

While A and B will affect the size of the curve, the a:b ratio, along with δ (or the phase shift) will affect the shape of the curve.
